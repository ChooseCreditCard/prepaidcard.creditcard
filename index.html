How Prepaid Credit & Debit Cards Actually Work

Prepaid cards let you spend only the balance you’ve loaded — useful for budgeting, travel, online safety and avoiding debt. This guide covers how they work, how to top them up, and what fees to check.

Compare prepaid & other card types

What Is a Prepaid Card?

A prepaid card is a payment card that draws from money you load onto it in advance — not from a credit line and not from a bank account. It works on the Visa, Mastercard or similar networks, so you can use it anywhere those brands are accepted.

Because it isn’t a credit product, prepaid cards do not build credit history and cannot accumulate debt. For many users, this makes them ideal for budgeting, controlling spending, and safer online purchases.

How You Load Money Onto a Prepaid Card

Most prepaid cards support multiple loading methods. Common options include:

  • Bank transfer or direct deposit
  • Card-to-card transfers
  • Cash reloads at participating stores
  • Mobile app top-ups

Many products support instant loads, but some charge a fee or apply limits. For travel use, make sure the card supports international transactions and dynamic currency conversion avoidance.

Fees & Limitations to Watch Out For

Prepaid cards may include multiple small fees depending on the issuer. Typical examples include:

  • Reload fees (especially cash reloads)
  • Monthly maintenance fees
  • ATM withdrawal fees
  • Foreign exchange margins
  • Card inactivity fees

Because prepaid cards do not earn rewards and do not build credit, they should mainly be used when budgeting, online safety or controlled spending is your primary goal.
